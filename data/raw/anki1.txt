What are Darwin's three major propositions of evolution?	1. Species change over time (e.g. finch beak variation) 2. Descent with modification (homologous structure) 3. Natural selection explains adaptive change (Galápagos finches)
Distinguish between natural selection and evolution	Natural selection = mechanism (process) Evolution = outcome (change in allele frequencies over time)
State the five assumptions of Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium	No mutation No gene flow Infinite population size Random sampling No selection
Define and contrast bottleneck effect and founder effect	Bottleneck effect = sudden reduction in population size - loss of allels Founder effect = Few individuals colonize new area - reduced diversity
Define macroevolution vs microevolution	Macroevolution = large-scale changes above species level (fossil record, speciation) Microevolution = small-scale allel frequency changes within populations
Contrast sympatric vs allopatric speciation	Allopatric = geographic barrier splits population Sympatric = no physical barrier, isolation via ecological/behavioral mechanisms
Contrast prezygotic vs postzygotic isolation	Prezygotic = barriers before fertilization (temporal, behavioral, mechanical) Postzygotic = reduced hybrid viability/fertility (mule sterility)
Define the biological species concept	species = interbreeding natural populations producing fertile offspring; reproductive isolation seperates species
Define balancing selection	Natural selection that maintains genetic diversity by keeping multiple allels at higher frequency than chance (genetic drift)
Give an example of heterozygote advantage	Sickle-cell trait: HbA/HbS heterozygotes resistant to malaria
Define sexual selection: mate choice vs male conflict	Mate choice (inter-sexual): females select males, ornaments (e.g. widowbird tails) Male conflict (intra-sexual): males compete, armaments (e.g. elephant seals)
Define coevolution	Reciprocal evolutionary change between interacting species (e.g. plant-pollinator, predator-prey)
State Fick's Law and its factors	\[ Q = DA \frac{(P_1 - P_2)}{L} \] Diffusion depends on surface area, pressure gradient, distance
Why is countercurrent exchange efficient in fish gills?	Water and blood flow in opposite directions = maintain oxygen gradient across gill length
Contrast gas exchange in air vs water	Air: high \( O_2 \), low density, diffusion faster Water: Low \( O_2 \), dense/viscous, diffusion 8000\(\times \) slower, high energy cost
Compare insect, bird, and mammal respiratory systems	Insects: tracheae Birds: unidirectional airflow (air sacs, parabronchi) Mammals: tidal ventilation (mixing)
How do plants exchange gases without transport organs?	Diffusion via loosely packed cells (gas diffuse through air 1000 times faster than water). In many plant stems, these air filled spaces form aerenchyma (通气组织) Living cell located close to the surface Woody stems and shoots: lenticels (皮孔) replace stomata Wetland plants: air taken through one broken stem/dead plant reach other healthy sections through the rhizome network.
Name essential nutrients for plants	Macronutrients: N, P, K, Ca, Mg, S Micronutrients: e.g. Fe, Zn, Mn
Describe nitrogen fixation in plants	Bacteria in root nodules (Rhizobium 根瘤菌) use nitrogenase to convert \( N_2 \) to \( NH_3 \). Plants supply carbohydrates.
Contrast ammonia, urea, and uric acid excretion	Ammonia: low energy, toxic, aquatic Urea: moderate energy, mammals/amphibians Uric acid: high energy, low water, reptiles/birds
Describe the nephron and an adaptation in arid mammals	Nephron = glomerulus (肾小球) + tubule (肾小管) Dessert mammals: long Loop of Henle - concentrate urine
List three types of sensory receptors	Chemoreceptors (molecules), mechanoreceptors (forces), photoreceptors (light)
How does phytochrome ratio \( P_r:P_{fr}\) affect growth?	Sunlight - more \( P_{fr} \) - growth Shade - more \( P_r \) - shade-avoidance response
How does jet lag disrupt circadian rhythm?	Light cues reset SCN (suprachiasmatic nucleus 视交叉上核, hypothalamus). Rapid travel misaligns SCN with peripheral clocks - fatigue, discomfort
List four modes of heat exchange	radiation: Heat moves from warmer objects to cooler ones via the exchange of infrared radiation (what you feel when you stand in front of a fire).     convection: Heat exchanges with a surrounding medium such as air or water that flows over a surface (the wind-chill factor).     conduction: Heat flows directly between two objects at different temperatures when they come into contact (e.g., an icepack on a sprained ankle).     evaporation: Heat is transferred away from a surface when water evaporates on that surface (the effect of sweating).
Define BMR vs SMR	BMR: endotherms at rest, thermoneutral zone. SMR: ectotherms at rest, at specific environmental temperature.
Define iteroparity vs semelparity	Iteroparity = multiple reproductive events (e.g., humans, turtles) Semelparity = one event, adults die (e.g. salmon, agave)
Contrast r- and K-selected species	r-selected: many offspring, early reproduction, low parental care K-selected: few offspring, late reproduction, high parental care
Describe Type I, II and III survivorship curves	Type I: low early mortality, high rate (humans) Type II: constant mortality (birds) Type III: high early mortality, survivors live long (insects)
Define life history trade-off	Limited resources force organisms to allocate between survival, growth, and reproduction
Define a population and its four properties	Population = individuals of same species in one area Properties: size, boundaries, distribution, structure
What are the assumptions of mark-recapture?	Marks not lost Marking harmless No trap bias Closed population

What parameters are needed to estimate the impact of gene flow?	1. Genetic difference between populations 2. Migration/hybridization rate (m)
"What is ""genetic rescue"" in conservation?"	Increasing genetic diversity by introducing individuals from another population (e.g. Florida panther + Texas puma)
Give examples of adaptive introgression	Flower timing in plants, mimicry in butterflies, black coat in wolves/dogs, human traits from Neanderthals
Which reproductive isolation is stronger, sympatric or allopatric? Why?	Sympatric. Sympatric species must evolve stronger barriers to prevent hybridization, while allopatric species are geographically separated.

physiological vs behavioral thermoregulation?	Physiological = internal (sweating, shivering) - mainly endotherms Behavioral = actions (basking, burrowing) - mainly ectotherms
Which has higher metabolic rate: 120 g fish or 100 g rabbit?	Rabbit. Endotherms have inherently higher metabolism than ectotherms of similar size

What organelles are responsible for sensing gravity in roots? How does it regulate gravitropism? How does differential growth create root bending?	Amyloplasts 淀粉体 (statoliths 平衡石) in statocytes of the root cap settle to the lower side of statocytes, triggering hormone redistribution (auxin), leading to asymmetric growth.  Growth is inhibited on the lower side (more auxin) and stimulated on the upper side, causing roots to bend downward (positive gravitropism). Explain thigmotropism.	Growth response to touch. Negative in roots (growing away from obstacles), positive in tendrils (curling around supports).
How does differential cell growth cause positive thigmotropism in tendrils?	Positive thigmotropism is when a plant tendril grows towards a physical touch stimulus. On the side in contact with the object, cells elongate less On the opposite side, cells elongate more This differential growth cases the tendril to bend and wrap around the object
How does negative thigmotropism occur in roots?	Negative thigmotropism is when a root grows away from a physical obstacle it touches. On the side in contact with the obstacle, cells elongate more On the opposite side, cells elongate less This differential growth bends the root away from the obstacle

What is the difference between exponential and logistic population growth?	Exponential growth occurs when resources are unlimited, producing a constant per capita growth rate. Logistic growth slows as population size approaches carrying capacity (K), where resources become limiting.
Define carrying capacity (K).	The maximum population size that an environment can sustain indefinitely given available resources
Give examples of density-dependent and density-independent factors that control population growth.	Density-dependent: competition, predation, disease. Density-independent: temperature, storms, pollution.
What is environmental stochasticity?	Random, unpredictable environmental variations (e.g., droughts, harsh winters) that cause population fluctuations regardless of density.
Why are small populations more susceptible to demographic stochasticity?	Random differences in births and deaths have a larger proportional effect, potentially leading to extinction even if average growth rates are positive.
What is a metapopulation?	A group of spatially separated populations linked by dispersal.
How do source and sink populations contribute to metapopulation persistence?	Source populations produce excess individuals that colonize sink patches, maintaining regional stability despite local extinctions.
Give examples of prey adaptations to avoid predation.	Camouflage, toxins, warning coloration, mimicry, group behavior, alarm calls.
Define competition in ecology.	An interaction where species vie for the same limiting resource, reducing growth, survival, or reproduction of at least one participant.
Contrast interference and exploitation competition.	Interference: direct prevention (e.g., territorial exclusion). Exploitation: indirect use of shared resources; efficiency determines outcome.
Explain how competition shapes zonation patterns on rocky shores.	Upper-tolerant species (Chthamalus) survive higher zones; competitive dominants (Semibalanus) occupy lower zones, excluded from upper by desiccation.
Define dispersal.	The movement of individuals from their birthplace to a new location for breeding.
Differentiate dispersal and migration.	Dispersal = one-way movement; Migration = regular, seasonal, two-way movement.
Define symbiosis.	A close and long-term interaction between two species, which may be mutualistic (互利共生), commensal (偏利共生), or parasitic.
Distinguish between parasitism, mutualism, and commensalism.	Parasitism: one benefits, other harmed. Mutualism: both benefit. Commensalism: one benefits, other unaffected.
Give examples of microparasites and macroparasites.	Microparasites: viruses, bacteria. Macroparasites: tapeworms, nematodes.
What is the difference between monoxenic and heteroxenic life cycles?	Monoxenic: single host species. Heteroxenic: multiple hosts (intermediate + definitive).
Give an example of an obligate mutualism.	Yucca plant and yucca moth — both depend on each other for reproduction.
What does the stress-gradient hypothesis predict?	Competition dominates in low-stress environments; mutualism increases in high-stress environments.
Define a pest or weed in ecological terms.	A species with populations perceived as too abundant or damaging, usually due to human interests.
What is the enemy release hypothesis?	Introduced species often thrive because they escape their natural predators and pathogens.
Define classical biological control.	The intentional introduction of natural enemies from a pest’s native range to control its population.
Contrast inoculative and inundative biological control.	Inoculative: few individuals released to reproduce and establish. Inundative: many released for immediate, short-term control.
Define endemic vs epidemic diseases.	Endemic: constant in a region. Epidemic: sudden outbreak affecting many individuals.
What traits allow a pathogen to drive extinction?	High virulence, multi-host infection, frequency-dependent transmission, long-lived infective stages.
Define an ecological community.	A group of potentially interacting species that coexist in space and time.
List the three “filters” determining community composition.	Regional species pool (evolution), dispersal, abiotic and biotic filters (niches).
Distinguish between fundamental and realised niches.	Fundamental niche = potential physiological range. Realised niche = actual range after biotic interactions.
How can disturbance influence community composition?	It resets succession, favors early colonizers, and alters competitive interactions.
What does the Shannon index measure?	Species diversity combining richness and evenness: \( H = - \sum_{i=1}^{S} p_i \ln(p_i) \)
How can species diversity enhance community stability?	Through functional complementarity and asynchronous species responses buffering fluctuations.
Differentiate energy flow from nutrient cycling.	Energy flows one-way and is lost as heat; nutrients cycle between biotic and abiotic pools.
Define Net Primary Production (NPP).	NPP = GPP – respiration; the amount of carbon remaining after plant respiration.
What drives global NPP patterns?	Temperature, light, and moisture — highest in tropical rainforests, lowest in deserts and poles.
Define a trophic cascade.	A process where changes at one trophic level cause cascading effects on lower levels (e.g., wolves → elk → vegetation).
Compare top-down vs bottom-up control.	Top-down: predators regulate lower levels. Bottom-up: nutrients/energy regulate higher levels.
What is eutrophication?	Nutrient enrichment causing algal blooms, oxygen depletion, and fish kills.
Which biogeochemical cycle is most biologically mediated?	The nitrogen cycle — driven by bacterial fixation, nitrification, and denitrification.
What causes biodiversity loss?	Habitat destruction, climate change, invasive species, overexploitation, pollution, and disease.
Why do small populations lose genetic diversity?	Inbreeding depression and genetic drift.
Why does biodiversity loss matter to humans?	It reduces ecosystem services (e.g., food, water, pollination) and cultural/psychological wellbeing.
List the “Evil Quartet” of extinction drivers.	Habitat loss, invasive species, overexploitation, co-extinction (+ disease, climate change).
What are the UN’s three main actions for biodiversity recovery?	Conservation/restoration, sustainable production/consumption, climate mitigation.
Compare the role of protected areas vs restoration.	Protected areas prevent further loss; restoration rebuilds degraded ecosystems.
Why is intensive species management important?	It prevents extinction when natural recovery is impossible (e.g., captive breeding).
